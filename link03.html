珍珠链战略


珍珠链战略（China's “string of pearls”），是国外有些别有用心的媒体声称中国正透过资助等各种方式取得军舰海外停泊基地，主要包括巴基斯坦、孟加拉国、缅甸、柬埔寨以及泰国等国家的有关港口或机场，这些国家在地图上联系起来像一串珍珠，故有此名称。但是，中国这些的资助行为完全属于纯商业的运作，不参与主权的管理，因此，珍珠链战略是些别有用心的国家制造的一种威胁论调。

在20世纪90年代末，美国南方司令部撰写了一份机密报告，说中国正试图利用世界各地的贸易港口设施来控制“战略关卡”。此后，类似言论便不绝于耳。2005年初被曝光的美国国防部一份名为《亚洲的能源未来》的内部报告称，中国正采取一种“珍珠链”式的战略：“中国正在从中东到南中国海的海上航道沿线建立战略关系，表明了它保护中国能源利益并同时为广泛的安全目标服务的防御与进攻态势。”报告还列出了所谓“珍珠链战略”中的六颗“珍珠”：首当其冲的是瓜达尔港。报告称，中国正在那里修建一个新海军基地。

“珍珠链”一词频频出现在西方媒体对中国海军的报道中。2005年初曝光的五角大楼一份名为《亚洲的能源未来》的内部报告中首次提出中国海军正采取“珍珠链”式战略的说法，报告列出了所谓“珍珠链战略”中的几颗“珍珠”：首当其冲的是巴基斯坦的瓜达尔港，其他“珍珠”还包括孟加拉国、缅甸、柬埔寨以及泰国等。在美国一些战略学者看来，中国要发展蓝水海军就不可能没有补给基地，于是他们炮制出所谓中国的“珍珠链”战略，声称中国正透过资助等各种方式取得军舰海外停泊基地。

中国决定借钱给斯里兰卡修机场和铁路的消息3月11日在国际上引起重重猜忌。在印度及西方一些人眼中，中国向这个去年刚结束内战的小岛国慷慨解囊是奇怪的，除了中国在瞄准自郑和时代以后一直遥不可及的印度洋，任何其他的解释都被他们看成中国在言不由衷。西方舆论经常把所谓中国的“珍珠琏战略”挂在嘴边，意思是中国一直悄悄地在筹划从东亚前往中东的水路上构筑支持海上行动的一连串堡垒，而其中斯里兰卡是新近最亮的一粒珠子。在一些印度舆论领袖的偏执想象中，这条“珍珠链”更像是要套住印度脖子的绳索。对这样的误读，中国一些战略学者感觉很无奈，他们坦言中国对南亚及印度洋上的战略考量并不像外界想得那么深，中国的力量远远支撑不起这样奢侈的战略野心。他们尤其抱怨印度明里暗里阻挠中国与斯里兰卡的合作是短视的。中国学者胡仕胜11日对《环球时报》说，中国在斯里兰卡援建的项目，甚至有一些是当地政府在请求印度援助遭拒后才找到中国的，而对斯里兰卡影响最大的国家过去和现在都是印度，印度没有必要这么不自信。

　　“中国展示的肌肉比印度多”

　　据法新社11日报道，斯里兰卡外交部本周三宣布，中国向斯里兰卡政府提供2.9亿美元贷款，用于建造一座机场并扩建该国的铁路系统。众所周知，这样的基础建设中国是长项。

　　《印度教徒报》11日用大量篇幅介绍中斯最新贷款协议的内容和建设动向，称“中国使用金钱武器”，“给斯里兰卡基础设施建设打了一剂强心针”。该报忧心忡忡地写道，斯中经济关系最近“迅猛发展”，中国已经超越亚洲开发银行和世界银行，成为斯里兰卡最大的经济援助国。

　　英国广播公司(BBC)强调，中国要在斯里兰卡建造的机场的位置，就在中国援建的“巨大港口”汉班托特旁边(该港口被西方认为是中国珍珠链战略的一环)。报道说，印度“是斯里兰卡的邻居，也是中国的对手”，一些印度官员说他们“害怕北京试图通过经济援助的方式削弱印度在该地区的影响力”。

　　马来西亚《太阳报》11日报道的标题是“斯里兰卡和中国做大买卖”，强调斯里兰卡兴建第二座国际机场的钱“大部分是中国掏”，印度和中国针对斯里兰卡重建合同的竞争十分激烈。

　　美国“Trans World News”网站11日报道说，这又是一个让印度在该地区损失影响力的举动。现在的情况是：“中国在这个地区展示的肌肉比印度多”。

印度洋上的战略要塞

　　斯里兰卡是印度南部一个岛国，有人形容它是从印度脸颊滑落印度洋的一颗泪珠。它的战略位置非常重要，位于三洲(亚欧非)两洋(太平洋、印度洋)之间，扼守亚丁湾至马六甲海峡的能源与贸易生命线。自古以来，斯里兰卡就是印度洋地区的交通和贸易要冲。斯里兰卡拥有1400多公里黄金海岸线，良港众多，军事地位也十分突出。二战期间，斯曾是英国遏制日本海军西进印度洋的战略要塞。

　　斯里兰卡曾沦为英国殖民地150年，1948年独立后，又因泰米尔和僧伽罗两大族群的激烈矛盾陷入长达26年的内战，直到2009年5月18日，斯里兰卡国防部宣布攻陷泰米尔“猛虎”组织最后据点。

　　斯里兰卡与印度关系紧密，斯境内两大民族均起源于印度，直至今天，南亚各国民众要去斯里兰卡，几乎都必经印度南部泰米尔纳德邦首府金奈，甚至有一名印度总理死于斯里兰卡内战：1991年，时任印度总理的拉吉夫甘地死于“猛虎”组织的“人体炸弹”。

　　中国和斯里兰卡的友谊虽然也源远流长，但跟印度没法比。今年1月斯里兰卡大选结束后，BBC曾经这样评价斯里兰卡与中印的关系：“中国和斯里兰卡都喜欢说自己是对方的老朋友，早在上世纪50年代时就签订了大米换橡胶协议。有观察人员认为，在内战时期斯里兰卡政府跟中国走得非常近”，但“由于文化、历史和宗教等各方面的原因，斯里兰卡会始终依赖印度。实际上这次竞选总统的两名候选人都在争取得到印度支持。现在的斯里兰卡是在西方、印度和中国这三个巨人中间找到平衡的立足点，而不是过分亲近中国。”

　　印度不满中国进入斯里兰卡

　　但在斯里兰卡问题上，印度一直对中国很防备。早在2007年，印度国家安全顾问纳拉亚南就强烈敦促斯里兰卡“不要向中国和巴基斯坦购买武器装备”，他还说，印度是地区大国，斯里兰卡需要的任何武器装备，印度都有能力提供。2009年4月，中国公开支持斯里兰卡政府打击“猛虎”组织，激起印度对中国的戒备，印度内政部长齐丹巴兰在接受《印度斯坦时报》专访时，竟称中国“在斯里兰卡混水摸鱼”。

　　2009年5月“猛虎”组织瓦解之后，纳拉亚南立即对斯里兰卡展开闪电访问，对帮助斯战后重建表示出浓厚兴趣，印度还向斯里兰卡派兵执行排雷任务，与此同时，斯里兰卡取消了与中国及巴基斯坦的武器购买合同。

　　《印度时报》评论说，印度最终希望抗衡中国在斯里兰卡日益增大的影响力。该报称，中国逐步成为斯里兰卡最大的防务产品提供商，而且也“谋求并得到”印度洋的一个珍贵港口―――汉班托特港，“完全是因为后者在印度洋的战略地位”。

　　“美国输不起斯里兰卡”

　　正如路透社3月10日写道，内战结束后，对斯里兰卡投资显现出“赢利吸引力和战略价值”。政治、经济日趋稳定的斯里兰卡吸引的绝不仅仅是印度和中国的目光。2009年12月，美国参议院外交关系委员会发表报告称，随着斯里兰卡从战争中恢复，华盛顿应该加强同它的联系，否则会面临将其“输给”中国和其他国家的危险。报告警告称，“美国输不起斯里兰卡。如果美国只是一味批评斯里兰卡的人权而不与之接触，那么这个国家就会像缅甸一样最终‘与世隔绝’”，“虽然人道主义关切仍很重要，但是美国的斯里兰卡政策不能只是一根筋”。

　　据路透社报道，2009年5月，“猛虎”组织被打败后，时任美国驻斯里兰卡大使罗伯特布莱克立即被提升为美国负责南亚与中亚事务的助理国务卿，据说这一提升的原因是奥巴马政府对斯里兰卡局势进行再评估的结果，认为中国与印度在该国的影响“已经太强大了”。

　　西方对中国和印度在南亚的影响力无疑有些眼红。因此当中国帮助斯里兰卡修建新机场的消息传出时，西方媒体的反应似乎比印度媒体还要大。BBC一方面称“中国正试图削弱印度在该地区的影响力”，一方面还颇有意味地说：这些工程都由中国承包商负责建造并使用大量中国工人，“斯里兰卡公司是有一些不满的，当地工人也觉得自己被遗忘了”。

　　不仅在斯里兰卡，中国在南亚任何一个国家的行动，都会被印度媒体和西方媒体紧紧盯住。过去，它们在报道中国帮助援建巴基斯坦的瓜达尔港、孟加拉国的吉大港和缅甸的实兑港等南亚项目时，都认为这是中国在印度洋上实施“珍珠链”战略一部分。中国瓜达尔在建港口项目进行中，西方媒体就带有挑拨性地报道说，“中国在印度洋沿岸建军港，准备在霍尔木兹海峡全面实现海军存在”。印度媒体则跟着西方大声疾呼“中国准备与印度争夺印度洋”。而当中巴恰希马核电站项目的第一期和第二期完成后，西方媒体则声称，中国正在秘密帮助巴基斯坦建立带有军事用途的核设施，而印度报纸则添油加醋地说：“中国正在秘密帮助巴基斯坦制造核武器”。这样的例子近年来还有很多。

　　“不做二流大国”政策的误导

　　现在，印度正千方百计改善与斯里兰卡的关系，曾任印度驻华大使的印度外交秘书拉奥琪几天前还率高级外交官代表团访问了斯里兰卡。

　　上海国际问题研究中心南亚中亚研究所所长王德华11日在接受《环球时报》采访时表示，600年前郑和下西洋就是促进和平，如今是中国历史上第二次进入印度洋。虽然有西方学者认为谁控制了印度洋就控制了世界。不过，中国的目标是要发展和谐印度洋。印度方面对中国援建斯里兰卡过分猜忌根本上还是因为“冷战思维”在作怪，这样就会让印度误解中国与斯里兰卡以及周边国家的友好关系，就不会理解帮助斯里兰卡恢复稳定与发展是世界共同的利益诉求。自尼赫鲁以来，印度国策强调“不做二流的大国”，其实一直让印度对世界和地区的发展形势产生误判。

　　中国现代国际关系研究院研究员胡仕胜11日对《环球时报》记者表示，中国对斯里兰卡的影响力绝对是与印度无法相提并论的。中国在斯里兰卡修建港口包括相应的一些基础设施，都是和平用途的，没有什么值得猜测的目的，印度媒体对这些事的夸大描述，显得有些迎合西方和别有用心。过去印度官方也有不少这样的猜忌，现在随着中印关系的进步，印官方对中国的类似指责已经很少了。

　　胡仕胜也表示，中国方面也需要保持理解的心态看待印度部分鹰派人士的言辞，理解习惯于将斯里兰卡当成自己“势力范围”的印度的感受，如同自己家门口来了一个“新人”，多少感到不舒服。中国不必过多计较这些言辞。从长期看，中国在印度周边以及印度在中国周边都会不断有新的影响力，这是两国各自崛起的必然趋势，在这个趋势上，中印彼此都要学会相互适应

美国驻日本军事基地

中日再战推演

　美国《外交政策聚焦》7月18日文章，原题：中国“珍珠链”真有其事？

　　据说，中国的“珍珠链”是由港口和机场建设项目、外交关系及军力现代化组成的。这些“珍珠”从中国大陆沿海延伸到近来升级的海南岛军事基地，经由南海到马六甲，直至印度洋及波斯湾沿岸。它们还包括位于孟加拉国的吉大港、缅甸实兑的深水港及位于巴基斯坦瓜达尔的潜在海军基地――这一切都代表中国地缘政治影响力或军事存在。

瓜达尔（Gwadar）位于巴基斯坦西南沿海，靠近波斯湾的霍尔木兹海峡，在盛产石油的中东地区，人口稠密的南亚和新兴经济和资源丰富的中亚地区之间。瓜达尔港的修建，预计会带来数十亿美元的收入，创造2万个就业岗位。目前有一新兴建的港口，可以连通南亚及西亚。

　　自2004年问世以来，“珍珠链”理论一直争议不断。尽管这种说法很流行，但目前猜测成分多于事实。中国确实在印度洋沿岸兴建商港，但尚未在那里建立任何海军基地，而是在实行美国官员所说的“落脚点而非基地”的战略。中国目前也尚无力将这些商港转化为基地，对珍珠链的恐惧掩盖了这一现实。

　　防务专家罗伯特・卡普兰2008年曾到访瓜达尔。他惊讶地发现，“那里夹在大海与沙漠之间，仿佛与世隔绝”。卡普兰认为瓜达尔安全形势脆弱，若修建从瓜达尔到中亚和中国的输油管，须等到阿巴局势稳定。否则，瓜达尔对崭露头角的中国海军虽是潜在的有用之地，但仍将是“死路一条”。眼下，珍珠链充其量只是中国的一个梦想。中国长远的海军现代化的大趋势也不应被珍珠链说法一叶障目。

　　珍珠链存在与否不重要，重要的是中国海军军力的投射及其背后的动机。北京大力打造海军，是基于国家安全的考虑，它涉及能源、海洋和领土安全。北京忙于全世界找石油，但眼下仍主要依赖中东。能源需求开始显著影响中国的战略思维和军事规划。北京希望掌控印度洋与太平洋之间的关键海上航线，办法是借助一连串海军设施和军事关系，由此也引发了外界的珍珠链辩论。

　　中国海军面临“马六甲困局”，该海峡目前超出其行动范围。因此，保障海上航线安全成为中国能源政策的一环，这也是中国打造海军及在建深水港等活动的主要出发点。解放军海军的活动和军力在扩大，但珍珠链理论并未能准确反映中国的国家安全现实。美国需要主动进行对华接触。简言之，美国应竭力使珍珠链停留在理论上，不去刺激中国将其变为现实。

原题：中国的珍珠链？ 一直以来，印度洋沿岸的一连串港口被称为中国的“珍珠链”。怀疑这种说法的人认为，中国并无意愿或能力在这些地方建海军基地。但珍珠链本来就不是用作海军基地的。笔者去瓜达尔、汉班托塔和中国人活跃的其他印度洋港口实地调查时就曾说过，那些可能是集商业、政治、战略及军事于一体的工程，且这些因素缺一不可。

除了日益依赖中东能源，中国越来越深地介入中东和非洲大陆的贸易、发展和资源开发，而印度洋是21世纪欧亚的海上指针。从这个意义上讲，瓜达尔、汉班托塔等港口，可以是中东与东亚之间的商业出货和仓储中转站。从战略上讲，它们为中国的新兴商业帝国提供了类似于19世纪加煤站的作用。

北京明白，使用这些港口，要靠与东道国的良好政治和经贸关系，因此在经济和政治领域积极主动帮助巴基斯坦、缅甸、斯里兰卡、孟加拉国等国。中国可以说是巴最可靠的盟友，也帮助斯里兰卡打赢内战，还与印度竞相援助孟加拉国。

考虑到北京与这些(印度洋)沿岸国家政经关系密切，协助修建并资助这些港口建设，若今后数年和数十年中国军舰及商船停靠或使用这些深水港口，那也是意料之中的事。

中国肯定无需为进入印度洋而主导西太平洋。它已有军舰远航至非洲之角打击海盗，且随着在较近海域的权力增加，中国升级行动进入印度洋也将是顺理成章的事。

Dan Drezner has a blog post up arguing that China‘s ‘string of pearls’ is a figment of journalists’ imaginations. The ‘string of pearls’ is the name given to China’s strategic investments in South and Southeast Asia, which, when plotted on a map, look awfully like a string of pearls encircling India. Pakistan is critical to this strategy, both because of its size and its location. Drezner is right to suggest that without the Sino-Pakistani link, the string of pearls theory doesn’t hold.

I’ve written about the string of pearls, and specifically about its Pakistani component, several times, for Forbes and other outlets, and I confess I’m not fully persuaded by Drezner’s critique.

1. Drezner depicts the Sino-Pakistani relationship as something that has arisen in response to the U.S. presence in the region and China’s growth:

“ For the past few years, a low level theme that occasionally pops into my news feed is the idea of greater Sino-Pakistani cooperation. Now this has a certain amount of realpolitik sense to it. The United States and Pakistan are not exactly on the best of terms, China is a rising power, they share a comon interest in containing India, yadda, yadda yadda. As a result, there has been the occasional press story about closer ties, which begets the invetiable U.S.-based blog posts about China expanding its “string of pearls” strategy of more deepwater ports in the Asia/Pacific region.

But China’s status as a Pakistani ally long predates the war in Afghanistan, or China’s rise as a major power. China and Pakistan established ties in the late 1940s, being among the first countries to recognize one another’s governments. The alliance deepened in the 1960s. China’s territorial dispute with India gave it common cause with Pakistan. The Sino-Soviet split prompted India’s abandonment of its non-aligned position in a 1971 treaty with the USSR, with China and Pakistan paired up in opposition. China’s arms sales to the Pakistani military and various defense cooperation agreements date from this period. Today, China is still a major supplier to the Pakistani Air Force, even if other divisions of the military enjoy closer cooperation with the United States. Arguing that there’s no Sino-Pakistani military link ignores this history; it would be more accurate to say that a longstanding Cold War-era link is under pressure in a post-Cold War world, as Pakistan has become more entangled with the U.S.

2. Drezner then goes on to highlight Gwadar, often depicted hysterically in the western, and Indian, press as a naval base for China. This is, as Drezner correctly points out, a depiction out of touch with the facts, but he jumps from the recognition that Gwadar isn’t a military base to the conclusion that it’s not a strategic asset at all, just a ‘fake pearl.’

A Chinese harbor development firm spent $200 million financing its development, it has been run for the last 5 years by the Port of Singapore Authority, and, the New York Times reported yesterday, a Chinese firm will be responsible for overseeing the port from 2013 onwards. When I reported on Gwadar in 2010, officials from both the Chinese developer and the PSA described its purpose as an economic one, a base for trade with the Persian Gulf. This week’s announcement is completely consistent with that interpretation.

Drezner rightly points out that there hasn’t been much traffic to Gwadar so far: the main reason for that is that Pakistan has failed to develop promised road and rail networks to connect Gwadar to the rest of the country. At one point, a frustrated China announced it would take responsibility for this infrastructure. That it hasn’t yet done so may reflect instability in Pakistan that prevents construction projects from going ahead, or it may reflect more fundamental Chinese skepticism about Pakistan, or both. But it’s got little to do with the non-military status of Gwadar; that’s how the port was always planned.

3. The fundamental point Drezner misses is that the ‘string of pearls’ isn’t about acquiring new military assets in Pakistan at all. It’s about building on existing military ties in Pakistan by A. investing in non-military assets in Pakistan and B. investing in both military and non-military assets elsewhere in the region.

On that front, Chinese activity has been mixed. China’s state-owned mining company is the dominant player in Pakistan’s minerals sector, and protects that advantage aggressively, even coming into conflict with the U.S. government over a copper and gold reserve in Balochistan. In 2010, China pledged an additional $35 billion worth of investment in Pakistani oil and gas, heavy industry and electronics. At the same time, a Chinese bank has prominently withdrawn its financial support from a gas pipeline from Iran to Pakistan that the U.S. government strongly opposes. Meanwhile, Chinese investments elsewhere in the region continue to grow. In a part of the world where regional trade is nearly non-existent, China’s bilateral arrangements with each country hold special significance.

In short, there is a case to be made that the Sino-Pakistani relationship C and with it the ‘string of pearls’ strategy C is weakening. That may be due to wariness about Pakistani instability or concerns about links between Pakistani militant groups and recent terrorist activity in China. It may be a sign that China is holding back until the U.S. presence in Pakistan is dialed down. It may reflect a judgment that the overall strategic goal of containing India is less urgent, given India’s own blundering when it comes to asserting itself in the region. But you’d have to ignore a lot of economic evidence to suggest that the strategy was never there at all.

China's String of Pearls?

By Robert Kaplan

February 15, 2013

The New York Times recently reported that China apparently has agreed to take over the operations of a $200 million port it built for Pakistan in Gwadar, on the Indian Ocean close to the Iranian border and close to the entrance to the Persian Gulf. We'll see if this actually happens. If it does, it will be geopolitically significant. To a greater degree than other Indian Ocean ports that China has either built, helped finance or upgraded, Gwadar is plagued by considerable security problems, as I found out during a visit there in 2008. It is in a remote region of Pakistan affected by Baloch separatism. And Baloch leaders I interviewed specifically threatened the building of a Chinese pipeline from Gwadar. Some years ago, China sat back and let a Singaporean firm run the new Gwadar port. At roughly the same time, Beijing shelved plans to build a nearby refinery. In 2011, when Pakistan publicly asked China to take over the port from the Singaporeans, China said no. China already has the Pakistani port of Karachi effectively for use, notes Indian analyst C. Raja Mohan. China has clearly been hedging its bets on Gwadar.

China's ostensible willingness to suddenly take the next step in Gwadar comes while the United States is in the process of pulling out of Afghanistan. Were Afghanistan to even partially stabilize following the withdrawal of U.S. troops, it would conceivably open up supply routes connecting Gwadar to Central Asia, and ultimately to China. This would ease China's so-called "Malacca dilemma," in which China is too dependent on the Strait of Malacca (and the nearby Lombok and Makassar straits) for the importation of hydrocarbons from the Middle East. China is already building roads and railways into Central Asia and has demonstrated, in its construction of parts of the Karakoram highway in southwestern China and northern Pakistan, its ability to surmount logistical obstacles. A spur road and pipeline from Gwadar would allow for the transfer of energy and other goods from the Middle East without having to rely on the Strait of Malacca. China would also be able to establish a listening post near the Persian Gulf were it to actually operate Gwadar.

China's Malacca dilemma will be further alleviated this summer when Beijing expects another port and pipeline complex it has built to be operational: one taking oil and natural gas from the Indian Ocean off the Myanmar coast and transporting it from Kyaukpyu in Rakhine state across north-central Myanmar into southern China's Yunnan province. Myanmar's ethnic unrest poses a security challenge to the Chinese pipeline, perhaps a reason why China is active in mediating disputes between the Myanmar regime and one of its disaffected ethnic armies.

Given all of these projects, it is a great age in history to be a Chinese civil engineer. I observed firsthand in 2009 the building of the massive Hambantota port complex by the Chinese in southern Sri Lanka, in the north-central Indian Ocean, which officially opened the following year. China, according to reports, is also involved in the building of a mooring container terminal and a nearby bridge in the Bangladeshi port city of Chittagong, while also reportedly aiding in the construction of a deep-water port on Bangladesh's Sonadia Island in the northeastern Indian Ocean. Furthermore, the Henry L. Stimson Center in Washington, among other sources, reports that China is a potential lead investor in a port and pipeline project to transport oil from southern Sudan to Lamu, on Kenya's northern coast, in the western Indian Ocean.

This array of Indian Ocean ports has been dubbed China's "string of pearls." Those skeptical of the concept have said that China has little desire or capacity to build naval bases in these places. But the string of pearls was never properly meant to imply naval bases per se. It is a far subtler concept, as I fully elaborated upon in my 2010 book, Monsoon: The Indian Ocean and the Future of American Power. In my reporting trips to Gwadar, Hambantota and other Indian Ocean ports where the Chinese have been active, I described a possible commercial, political, strategic and lastly military venture, the constituent elements of which cannot be disaggregated. To be sure, we live in a post-modern world of eroding distinctions: a world where coast guards sometimes act more aggressively than navies, where sea power is civilian as well as military, where access denial can be as relevant as the ability to engage in fleet-on-fleet battle and where the placement of warships is vital less for sea battles than for diplomatic ones.

China, moreover, has historic roots in the Indian Ocean going back to the Song and early Ming dynasties. In particular, the Chinese regime recently spent a considerable amount of money in a public relations campaign to resurrect the legacy of Ming Dynasty Adm. Zheng He, who plied the seas between China, the East Indies, Sri Lanka, the Persian Gulf and the Horn of Africa in the 15th century. Zheng He's route took him to all the places where China is now involved in port projects: It is the same route that ships must take to bring Middle Eastern hydrocarbons to China.

Gwadar Port, the Latest of the Chinese “Pearls”

28 May 2013

Marco Giulio Barone

by securityobserver

By Marco Giulio Barone. The “String of Pearls” strategy was conceived for providing Beijing with the possibility of having access to raw materials and energy supplies from the Middle East and Africa through a series of choke points along South Asia, from mainland China to Port Sudan. But this strategy has shown some vulnerability because of India’s increasing naval interdiction in the Indian Ocean[i]. From a Chinese perspective, Gwadar port may represent the solution to this threat. In fact, as part of broader agreements between China and Pakistan, several major infrastructure pivoted to Gwadar will connect China directly to the Arabian Sea[ii]. Accordingly, if China succeeded in Gwadar, the United States and India may have to redraw their grand strategy in East Asia.

In a momentous decision, on January 30, 2013, Pakistan handed over the management of Gwadar port to a Chinese company, China Harbor Engineering[iii]. Gwadar is located in the south-west of Pakistan’s Balochistan province, at the juncture of three regions, Central Asia, the Indian Ocean and the Middle East, and is close to the key trading and oil transportation route of Hormuz. Pakistan purchased the Gwadar enclave from Oman in 1958[iv]. However, the harbor has been inactive for a long time. In 1973, following President Nixon’s visit to Pakistan, Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto offered Gwadar to the United States as a naval base for its fleet in exchange for developing a major seaport there through direct investment. The United States declined and as a consequence the harbor stayed inactive for almost thirty years. In 2002, China decided to revive the seaport by investing 200 million USD in infrastructure[v]. Following the completion, in 2007, the Government of Pakistan signed a long term agreement with PSA International (Singapore), one of the world’s largest port operators, for the development and operation of the tax-free port and duty-free trade zone[vi]. Nonetheless, Singapore has not invested much. Therefore Pakistan decided to drag China once more into the project after asking Singapore to repeal the agreement[vii]. In this second round of involvement, Chinese investments have been massive. Fitting out the seaport will cost 932 million USD and will allow Gwadar to triple its trade flow[viii]. Another significant aspect is the two oil terminals that will provide Gwadar with the capability of hosting oil tankers and moving up to 19 million tons of crude oil per year. Crude oil is expected to be refined at this port and then sent to China via land pipeline (Gwadar-Kashgar project)[ix].

Strategic value of Gwadar will be enhanced with the completion of multiple projects linked to the port’s energy security issues. It will be designed to become a strategic hub where oil and natural gas from Africa and the Persian Gulf are collected and transported to China. At the moment, the only viable line of communication between the two countries is the Karakorum Highway, the highest paved road in the world. Hence, a vital part of the port’s planned project is connecting the highway to Gwadar, creating a ground line of communication. The effectiveness of such a line has been questioned many times, since the highway runs across the two countries’ most instable areas, namely Baluchistan and Xinjiang. These two regions are known for violent riots against their respective central governments and have witnessed several terroristic attacks[x]. China is planning to reduce such vulnerability through two additional major infrastructure projects: a 3000 km railway road and the Iran-Pakistan pipeline. The first project is expected to follow the same path of the highway, thus connecting Gwadar to Kashgar. Building in the south-west of Pakistan’s Balochistan province in the mountains will be very expensive. Yet at the highest points the railway will cost about 30 million USD per kilometer and China seems willing to go for it[xi]. As for the second project, originally the Iran-Pakistan pipeline was supposed to provide India with fossil fuels through Pakistan, but theproject was never completed. Although inactive, China has forwarded a proposal for redrawing and extending the pipeline to reach Kashgar[xii].

Gwadar is seen as the latest choke point of China’s grand strategy. Gwadar adds a further pearl to China’s “”encirclement” strategy, which Beijing has used to create a series of sea lines of communication between mainland China and Port Sudan[xiii]. The bases and ports pass through several of the critical choke points that could threaten China’s energy supply, including the Strait of Mandeb, the Strait of Malacca, the Strait of Hormuz and the Lombok Strait. A huge amount of direct investment has been combined with the sound ability to obtain political agreements. Still, Chinese efforts seem inadequate for keeping its chain of facilities safe from Indian and/or American air and sea interdiction in cases of serious crisis[xiv]. For this reason Beijing is reluctant to accept the Pakistani offer of turning Gwadar into a naval base. In fact, a Chinese fleet in Gwadar is likely to remain isolated if the “string” were to be broken. Before extending any military influence in the Middle East, China needs to create strategic alternatives. Strengthening the direct line of communication Gwadar-Kashgar is likely to represent such an alternative, as well as creating worthy strategic redundancy (two ways, a ground line and a sea line for reaching the Middle East)[xv].

Contained by the tactics of the “String of Pearls”, India seems to have accepted its role as China’s counterpart in the Indian Ocean. Because of such a role, New Delhi gained legitimacy in strengthening its military power, especially the sea power. Gwadar project’s development represents a menace to that role if China succeeded in diminishing its strategic dependency on the “String of Pearls”[xvi]. To face this challenge New Delhi pledged funds to Iran, particularly in helping Teheran develop its seaport in Chahbahar. Although the United States wary of such developments on the Indian side, New Delhi is unwilling to become merely Washington’s vassal[xvii]. Rather, it wants to act as the protagonist in the region. In addition, Iran is now included in several international initiatives with India, Pakistan and China and therefore remains far from being totally isolated as the United States wishes.

Finally, what is Gwadar worth to Pakistan? Islamabad has become dependent on funds coming from its GLOCs (Ground Lines Of Communication) to Afghanistan. In the short term the government has to find economic alternatives and keep holding some international visibility after NATO’s withdrawal. At the same time, the country needs to be secured internally from sectarianism and political instability. Gwadar and the system of infrastructure expected would help the country respond to these needs, creating jobs, wealth and visibility. Hence, the Zardari government, before going to elections, wanted to play its last card by embracing the Chinese strategy[xviii]. Nevertheless it has to be stressed that the features of future Pakistani role in the region haven’t changed much. Islamabad is once again offering its territory as a bridge for someone else’s needs[xix].

Hungry of energy sources and raw materials, China is consolidating its outposts in the Middle East, Africa and the Indian Ocean. India, Pakistan and Iran are playing a prominent role in this move. Because of this policy the United States may be forced to change its attitude towards the Middle East. At the beginning of its pivot to Asia, the U.S. wanted to disengage from the Middle East. Chinese “pivot to the Middle East” is likely to force the United States to reconsider its commitment to the region.

China’s strategic interests in Pakistan’s port at Gwadar

March 24th, 2013

Author: Ghulam Ali, Institute of Information Technology, Islamabad

On 30 January 2013, the Port Singapore Authority (PSA) abandoned administrative control of Gwadar port in Pakistan ― five years into a 40-year agreement.

Pakistan has now handed over the ‘management and operation’ of Gwadar port to a Chinese company, and in another landmark decision, Pakistan has signed onto the IranCPakistan gas pipeline, much to the annoyance of the United States.

Commentators argue that both agreements are a political stunt; the incumbent government made these decisions shortly before it completed its five-year term in an attempt to restore its declining popularity. It has now left the next government to deal with the consequences.

Gwadar is located at the juncture of South Asia, Central Asia and the Middle East. It is close to the Iranian border and lies at the gateway to the Strait of Hormuz, a key world oil supply route. Some analysts argue the port could become China’s naval base in the Indian Ocean and enable Beijing to monitor Indian and US naval activities. The port has also been called the western-most link in China’s ‘string of pearls’ strategy.

How valid are these claims and what is the port’s real importance to China?

Pakistan purchased the small town of Gwadar from Oman in 1958, but did not begin work on a port there until 2002. Pakistan lacks ports for shipping and maritime security ― in successive conflicts, the Indian Navy quickly blockaded Karachi, severely limiting the Pakistan Navy’s manoeuvrability. Gwadar, a deep warm-water harbour 470 kilometres away from Karachi, seemed an ideal place for a new outlet to the Indian Ocean. At Pakistan’s request, China provided US$198 million for the first phase of the port, which was completed in 2006, but was lukewarm about further development. The project consists of three phases, so Pakistan is still waiting for investment to complete the remaining two.

The robust nature of ChinaCPakistan defence ties, and a number of interrelated developments therein, has catapulted the port to international attention. China is developing its own western region and has been building a network of roads in Pakistan, and intends to lay pipelines and a railway track. Pakistan offered China a ‘trade and energy corridor’ via Gwadar, linked to inland roads. The plan would see oil being imported from the Middle East, stored in refineries at Gwadar and sent to China via roads, pipelines or railway. Many Western and Indian analysts argue that China wants to gain a foothold in Gwadar for strategic purposes.

This analysis overplays Gwadar’s geo-economic importance and ignores important facts. Firstly, though China has built some roads in Pakistan, it still needs to lay thousands of kilometres of gas and oil pipelines and railway track in order to turn Gwadar to economic use. That will cost money, and China is reluctant to invest in this volatile part of the world. Secondly, Pakistan faces a low-scale insurgency in its Balochistan province ― where Gwadar is located and through which the proposed pipelines will pass. A number of feudal lords are opposed to large-scale foreign investment, fearing it will bring an influx of outsiders. They demand greater autonomy and royalties for the extraction of natural resources. Although China has developed local infrastructure, it is considered an ‘exploiter’. Some Chinese workers have in the past been victims of targeted attacks. Thirdly, the port has failed to draw any major business since its completion in 2006. The fact that the port remained unused was one reason the PSA withdrew. Unlike Islamabad’s tall claims about the port’s geo-economic significance, Beijing has taken a more cautious and realistic approach. China remains sceptical of the port’s profitability. Both in 2001, when it agreed to finance the first phase of the port, and in 2013, when it took over administrative control of the port, Pakistan had to drag Beijing into the project.

Gwadar is not the only option for the Chinese in the Indian Ocean. It is not even the most viable option. Beijing has developed Hambantota port in Sri Lanka and built a container port facility in Chittagong in Bangladesh. In Myanmar, Beijing has built roads, dams and pipelines, and is looking to the ports of Kyaukpyu and Sittwe, regardless of the fact that the latter is being built by India. Beijing intends to lay a pipeline from Kyaukpyu to Yunnan province. Chinese oil ships from the Middle East and Africa will cross the Bay of Bengal and unload at these ports, allowing oil to be piped to Yunnan. China appears more optimistic about the future of an ArakanaCYunnan pipeline than the GwadarCXinjiang pipeline because it considers Myanmar capable of protecting its assets.

Due to its strategic location, and because the strong military ties between China and Pakistan, Gwadar port has received excessive attention from the very beginning. Despite its being over a decade since China started construction of the first phase, no military-related activity has ever been observed there. If China intended to use a Pakistani port for naval purposes, Karachi, with its established military infrastructure, is an alternative that is available although Karachi has the strategic diswadvantage of proximity to India.

It is likely that China will develop the port quickly by making a bigger investment than the PSA, but its current interests appear commercial, aimed at securing its energy supplies. Moreover, Gwadar is just one of several options for Beijing, and due to the volatile security situation in the surrounding region it may not be China’s best bet. Gwadar is far from becoming a Chinese economic hub, let alone a security asset.

据英国广播公司网站9月20日报道，英国外交部耗资500万英镑（约合4900万元人民币）新成立的外交人员语言训练中心19日在黑格的主持下开幕。

报道介绍称，新成立的语言学校位于外交部在伦敦的总部内，有40间具备现代化设备的教室，每年将有1000名外交人员在此受训，学习70多种语言。

黑格表示，他的目标是将能说汉语普通话的英国外交人员人数提高40%，说西班牙语、葡萄牙语和阿拉伯语的人数提高20%。

报道称，英国外交人员在准备外派之前，都能获得外交部补助参加私人的语言课程。但黑格认为这样仍然不够，因为外语能力是“外交工作的一个基本技能”。

黑格说，前任英国工党政府关闭外交部自设的语言学校后，英国外交官外语能力就一直处于被忽略状态。

他说，关闭外交部自设的语言学校是一个极度“短视近利”的决定，挖空了外交部的核心，却只节省下一小笔钱。

报道指出，尽管黑格在外交部预算遭受大幅缩减的情况下大张旗鼓开办语言训练中心，但是派驻伦敦的欧洲外交人员却对此不抱太大希望。

一名欧洲外交官表示，“在法国和德国，外交部的新进人员必须要能说流利的英语和另一种外语。但是英国的外交部却不能设立这样的条件，因为英国大学毕业生能说外语的人太少，因此英国外交部才会需要自己成立语言学校，来弥补英国教育体制内外语学习严重缺乏的问题。

” 英国外交部高级官员认为，招募人才不严格设置外语能力并没有问题，因为条件放宽才能吸引专业能力更广泛的人才。

但一名欧洲外交官说，“英国人的本能反应是每个人都说英语，这一点基本上没有改变。”

威廉杰佛逊黑格（William Jefferson Hague，1961年3月26日－），台湾译作威廉海格，港澳译作夏チ郑英国保守党政治家，下议院议员，现任外交大臣。

外交及联邦事务大臣（Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs，简称外交大臣或外相，即Foreign Secretary）， 他生于约克郡的Rotherham镇。先后就读于 Ripon Grammar School 和 Wath Comprehensive School。他父母经营一家软饮料制造公司，他在学习假期经常到那里帮忙工作。

1977年，16岁的他在保守党的大会上发表演讲成为当时英国的一大新闻。

他先就读于牛津大学Magdalen学院的哲学、政治与经济学，获得一等毕业学位。他曾是牛津大学保守协会(OUCA)的主席。后来他获得欧洲工商管理学院工商管理硕士，后来为麦肯锡公司做管理咨询工作，在那里Archie Norman(英国商人与政治家)成为他的良师益友。

政治生涯

1989年在补选中首次当选英国下议院议员。1997年英国大选，保守党惨败，他其后接替约翰梅杰出任党魁，兼任反对党领袖。2001年英国大选，保守党再度惨败，他辞去党魁一职，成为自1921年以来首位未出任首相的保守党党魁。2010年保守党和自民党的联合政府上台后，他出任外交大臣。

据《t望东方周刊》

环球时报驻香港特约记者凌德 英国外交及联邦事务部国务大臣施维尔日前在港媒上撰文，称英国随时准备为香港普选提供支援。此事暴露英国干预香港事务“已赤膊上阵”，令各界哗然。为警示香港市民，有港媒日前揭露了自回归以来英国在香港进行的大量间谍活动。

据香港《大公报》、《文汇报》等媒体16日报道，英国情报机构在香港的活动，不仅没有在香港回归后消停，反而不断加强。众多退役、现役的英国军情六处驻港特工，有的以领事身份，有的以经济文化等部门职员身份在港服务于“英国的需要”。他们的“行动中心”，目前位于英国驻港总领事馆内。该馆于1996年建成，其设计者为英国建筑师泰瑞法雷尔，此人正是军情六处总部大楼的设计者，深谙情报机构建筑的游戏规则。

报道称，早在1984年《中英联合声明》落实香港主权于1997年归还中国后，港英情报部门“政治部”就部署变身，由官方转为非官方、由地上转为地下。在回归前数年，就在港英政府保安科下设神秘D组，实际上是个小型的军情六处组织。香港回归16年来，该组织不断在香港进行渗透与间谍活动，对香港特区政府乃至中国政府造成严重威胁。

《大公报》称，与美国“依赖高科技”相比，英国情报机构更注重“人的资源”，即十分注重对关键人物的收买与策反，“放长线钓大鱼”。为了达到目标，长期盯住关键人物，时间可以长达数年甚至十数年之久。正是这种情报运作方式，令英国当局可以在十分低调、不易被察觉的情况下，获取大量核心情报。此外，英国情报机构在香港还有一招“杀手锏”，即拥有大量政府高级公务员的绝密资料，包括个人污点、个人升迁轨迹等等。在回归前一年，此类资料并未在香港销毁，而是运回了英国。通过此类数据，长期控制着一批港英过渡的高级官员。而一旦有必要，则利用在港的喉舌媒体，发放不利消息以打击目标。前发展局局长麦齐光因骗领房屋津贴被控，但所有指控证据是源自数十年前的公务员资料，拥有此等能力的，应该只有英国当局。

《文汇报》称，香港回归16年来，一大批隐蔽身份的军情六处特务回流香港，渗入政党、团体、司法界、商会、传媒、政府要害部门，抢夺香港管治权。针对英国在港的间谍活动，《大公报》提醒说，香港市民对此需保持清醒认识，更要保持警觉，如果国家利益因此受损，香港也不可能独善其身。
